HISTORY

Javanese coffee is coffee originating from the island of Java in Indonesia. This coffee is so famous that the name Java became the identity name for coffee. Indonesian Javanese coffee does not have the same shape as coffee from Sumatra and Sulawesi, the taste is also not as rich as coffee from Sumatra or Sulawesi because most Javanese coffee is processed wet (wet process). Even so, some Javanese coffees emit a distinctive thin aroma of spices. Java coffee has a low acidity combined with soil conditions, air temperature, weather, and humidity. The most famous Javanese coffees are Jampit and Blawan. Old Javanese coffee beans (called old-brown) are large, and low in acid content.

In 1696 the Mayor of Amsterdam Nicholas Witsen ordered the VOC commander on the Malabar Coast, Adrian van Ommen to bring coffee seeds to Batavia (now Jakarta). The coffee seeds were tested for the first time on the private land of the VOC Governor-General Willem van Outhoorn in the area now known as Pondok Kopi, East Jakarta. The first harvest at Pondok Kopi was immediately sent to Hortus Botanicus Amsterdam. Biologists at Hortus Botanicus Amsterdam are amazed by the quality of Javanese coffee. According to them, the quality and taste of Javanese coffee surpassed any coffee they had ever known. Scientists immediately sent samples of Javanese coffee to various botanical gardens in Europe. Louis XIV's Royal Botanical Garden was one of those that received samples of Javanese coffee. The French immediately reproduced the consignment samples and sent them to their colonies for cultivation, including Central and South America. The coffee trade was indeed very profitable for the VOC, but not for coffee farmers in Indonesia at that time because of the implementation of the cultivation system. Over time, the term a Cup of Java appeared in the western world, this suggests that Indonesian coffee is synonymous with Javanese coffee, although there are still other delicious coffees such as Sumatran coffee and Sulawesi coffee. Coffee grown in Central Java is generally Arabica coffee. One of the Javanese coffees in Central Java comes from Tawangmangu, where the existing coffee type S line or original Dutch heritage. The Java coffee that was developed is thought to have existed since Tawangmangu was chosen by the Dutch who chose the slopes of Mount Lawu as a place to live as well as develop tea and coffee plantations. Currently, the population of Tawangmangu Arabica coffee is still around 4 hectares and Mahadri Coffee is starting to develop it. Meanwhile, in East Java, the types of Kayu Mas, Blewan, and Jampit coffees are generally Robusta coffee. In the mountainous area from Jember to Banyuwangi there are many Arabica and Robusta coffee plantations. Jember has been known worldwide as an area that produces quality and delicious Javanese coffee. The production of Javanese coffee from the world-famous Arabica coffee type has made many Javanese entrepreneurs successful in coffee trading. The price of Arabica coffee which is mostly produced in Java is more expensive than Robusta coffee. In fact, many countries in the world, especially America and Europe, call coffee identical to Java. Coffee production from Indonesia is the 3rd largest in the world.
